Stop 1: Lourdes Day Care Centre

Lourdes Day Care Centre

Lourdes Day Care Center is a hub of activity for the young of heart in Dublin's North East inner city.

Big Jim

Tess from Lourdes Day Care Centre remembers attending rallies given by Jim Larkin on O’Connell Street as a child. She also remembers Big Jim coming into her workplace as a teenager, to defend worker’s rights.

Dublin Town in 1962

Peggy and Tess from Lourdes day Care Centre sing about the landmarks of their childhood: the Hill of Howth, Nelson's Pillar, Clery's, the Metropole, the Hal'penny Bridge, the mailboat sailing from North Wall and paper boys selling the Herald and Mail.

Stop 4: James Larkin

James Larkin

James "Big Jim" Larkin was a worker's rights activist. An imposing figure with a powerful voice, he led unfairly treated workers to revolt against their bosses and form trade unions, resulting in the 1913 Lockout.

Jim Larkin: The Statue

Jim Larkin’s son, Denis, spoke at length to the sculptor Oisin Kelly before the life like statue was made of Larkin. It was erected on O’Connell Street directly across from Clerys shop. Patrick Kavanagh and Seán O’Casey contributed excerpts from their own works, pertaining to Larkin, to the plinth of the Statue.

Interview with Stella McConan

Students from Larkin Community College interview Stella Mc Connon, Jim Larkin’s granddaughter, about her memories of her grandfather. Stella remembers her grandfather’s funeral and his care of the families of the inner city.

Stop 5: Daniel O'Connell

Daniel O'Connell

After O’Connell’s funeral in 1847, a fund was started to raise money to build a monument to celebrate the life and works of the ‘Emancipator’. Over thirty figures on the monument symbolise aspects of O’Connell’s life.

O'Connell Statue

John Henry Foley started design of the O’Connell Monument and it was completed by the Thomas Brock. It is in three sections.

Daniel O'Connell's Duel

Students re-enact the duel that resulted in the death of John D’Esterre at the hands of Daniel O’Connell. D’Esterre was slighted by O’Connell and challenged him to a duel.

Museum as Barracks

The National Museum at Collins Barracks was originally built as a soldiers’ barracks in the 18th Century and remained such until 1922 when the Irish army took it over until 1997.

Museum as Museum

The National Museum of Ireland, Decorative Arts and History Museum opened as a Museum in 1997 and the Military History wing opened in 2007.
